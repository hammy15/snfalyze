# Company Analysis: Eduro Healthcare
> Dev Intelligence Report | Strategic M&A Assessment

## Company Overview

- **Ownership Type:** Founder-operated
- **Structure:** Multi-state founder-led healthcare operator
- **States:** 8 states
- **Facility Count:** 37-41 facilities (range reflects uncertainty in current count or pending openings/closures)
- **Beds:** Pending deeper analysis
- **Asset Type:** SNF (skilled nursing facilities)
- **Notable:** 3 of Eduro's 8 operating states are Cascadia home states -- significant geographic alignment on a partial basis

## Strategic Assessment

- **Cascadia Fit:** Moderate-to-strong on a carve-out basis. While the full 37-41 facility portfolio spans 8 states (likely including non-strategic geographies), 3 states overlap with Cascadia home states. A carve-out of the home-state facilities (estimated 12-17 operations) would be highly strategic.
- **Portfolio Impact (Full):** Adding 37-41 operations to Cascadia's 58 would push total to 95-99 operations. Significant but does not cross the 120-130 IPO threshold alone.
- **Portfolio Impact (Carve-out):** Adding 12-17 carve-out operations to Cascadia's 58 would bring total to 70-75 operations. Meaningful but incremental.
- **IPO Narrative:** A full acquisition diversifies geographically across 8 states -- potentially too dispersed. A carve-out of home-state facilities strengthens the PNW/home-state density narrative.
- **Cluster Potential:** High for home-state carve-out. Adding 12-17 facilities in 3 Cascadia home states deepens existing clusters. Low for non-overlapping states where Cascadia has no infrastructure.
- **Carve-Out Strategy:** Dev's recommended approach -- buy the best assets (home-state facilities), option on the remainder. This reduces integration risk while capturing the strategic value.

- **Estimated EBITDAR:** Pending deeper analysis
- **Carve-Out Valuation:** Would need facility-level P&L to price the home-state subset independently. Carve-outs typically transact at a slight discount to portfolio deals due to seller's remaining portfolio becoming less attractive.

## Seller Dynamics

- **Ownership Motivation:** Founder-operated. Motivation depends on founder age, succession plans, and liquidity needs. Founders who have built a 37-41 facility platform across 8 states may be approaching a natural liquidity event or may want to continue operating indefinitely.
- **Decision Maker:** Founder/CEO -- single decision-maker simplifies negotiation but founder attachment to the business can extend timelines.
- **Negotiation Leverage:** Cascadia can present a carve-out as a win-win: founder retains the non-overlapping operations while monetizing the Cascadia-adjacent facilities. This may be more palatable than a full sale to a founder who wants to keep operating.
- **CEO Status:** Active founder-operator (not a pre-sale vacancy signal)
- **PE Exit Window:** N/A -- founder-operated, not PE-backed
- **Carve-Out Complexity:** Founder may resist breaking up the portfolio. Shared services, corporate overhead allocation, and facility interdependencies complicate carve-outs. May need to offer a management agreement or transition services arrangement.

## Key Risks

- **Carve-Out Execution (High):** If Cascadia only wants 12-17 of the 37-41 facilities, the carve-out negotiation is complex. Shared overhead allocation, transition services, and brand separation all create friction.
- **Founder Attachment (High):** Founder-operators are emotionally invested. They may reject a partial sale or demand a premium for "breaking up" their platform.
- **Multi-State Complexity (Medium):** Even a carve-out spans 3 states with different regulatory environments. Full acquisition adds 5 more states Cascadia has no presence in.
- **Stranded Costs (Medium):** If Cascadia carves out home-state facilities, the seller's remaining portfolio loses scale benefits (corporate overhead now spread across fewer facilities). This may make the seller unwilling or force Cascadia to overpay.
- **Integration (Medium):** Founder-run operations often have founder-specific processes, culture, and relationships. Transitioning to Cascadia's platform requires careful change management.
- **Information Asymmetry (Medium):** 37-41 facilities across 8 states is a large, complex dataset. Due diligence burden is significant even for a carve-out.
- **Competitive (Low-Medium):** Other regional operators in those 3 home states may compete for the same facilities.

## Pipeline Positioning

- **Tier:** 3 (Opportunistic)
- **Confidence to Close:** 35-45%
- **Action Required:** OPPORTUNISTIC -- monitor for founder succession triggers, relationship-build, explore carve-out appetite informally before formal outreach
- **Timeline:** Pending deeper analysis -- founder-operated businesses sell on the founder's timeline, not the buyer's
- **Pipeline Context:** Ranks in the lower half of the active pipeline. Below Avamere (90.9%), Sapphire (78.8%), Ohana (~70%), and Haven Health (50-65%). Eduro's value is strategic but contingent on carve-out feasibility. A full 37-41 facility acquisition at the right price would be transformative (pushing Cascadia to ~100 ops), but the 8-state sprawl is problematic. The sweet spot is the 12-17 facility home-state carve-out at a fair price.
